# Ender 3 V3 KE — Frequently Asked Questions
machine: Ender 3 V3 KE
category: 3D printing — FAQ

Quick answers to common questions about 3D printing at the ICL.

## How long does a 3D print take?
It depends on the size and complexity:
- Small objects (keychains, small figurines): 30 minutes to 2 hours
- Medium objects (phone stand, small box): 2–5 hours
- Large objects: 5–12+ hours

Plan ahead if you have a deadline. Cura shows you the estimated print time after you slice your model.

## What's the biggest thing I can print?
The Ender 3 V3 KE can print up to **220mm × 220mm × 240mm** (about 8.7" × 8.7" × 9.4"). For anything larger, the object must be split into parts and assembled, or printed on the larger **CR-M4** printer (also in Plank 117).

## What material does it use, and which should I pick?
**PLA** is the default and the right choice for almost every beginner project — it's easy, safe, and low-odor. Other materials (PETG, ABS, TPU, ASA) are available but harder to print and not recommended for first-timers. Cura sets the right temperature automatically when you choose PLA.

## What colors are available?
Filament colors vary depending on what spools the ICL currently has in stock. Check the labeled filament storage area in Plank 117, or ask ICL staff what's available right now.

## Where is the filament kept?
In the labeled storage area in Plank 117. Spools load onto the holder on the back-right of the printer.

## Do I need a USB drive?
Yes — after you slice your model in Cura, you save the sliced file to a USB drive and plug it into the printer to start the print. Bring the file on a USB drive when you come to print.

## When is the lab open?
The ICL is open **24 hours a day, 7 days a week** to all Gettysburg students, faculty, and staff. The printer is in the Design Studio, Plank 117.

## Do I have to watch the whole print?
Not the whole time, but **stay for the first few minutes** to confirm the first layer goes down cleanly — that's when most failures happen. Once the first layer looks good and is sticking, you can step away. Write your name and contact info on the dry erase board while your print runs, and check on it periodically.

## What software do I use to prepare a model?
**Ultimaker Cura** (free) is the recommended slicer at the ICL. You open your model file in Cura, it converts it into instructions the printer understands, and you save that to a USB drive. Ask me "how do I 3D print something?" for the full walkthrough.

## I don't have a model file — can I still print?
Yes. You can download a ready-made model from a site like Thingiverse, or design your own for free in Tinkercad. Ask me "how do I design my own model?" and I'll walk you through it.

## Something went wrong with my print — what do I do?
Ask me about the specific problem (for example, "my print won't stick to the bed" or "my print looks like spaghetti") and I'll help you troubleshoot it.
